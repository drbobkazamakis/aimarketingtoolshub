The Real-AI Classifier Methodology

Every tool in the directory gets one of three tags: Real AI, Hybrid, or Marketing AI. This page is the rubric I use to make that call — and what I do when a tool sits between two categories.

R
Ruchika
PPC strategist · LinkedIn

The classifier exists because most “AI marketing tools” aren’t. The vendors know it; the buyers often don’t. This rubric is the answer to the question I get most: how do you actually tell?

The three categories, defined

Real AI — the system trains models on customer data

To earn the Real AI tag, a tool must satisfy all of the following:

  1. Custom machine-learning models are trained on each customer’s account data (not just “shared models trained across the customer base”).
  2. Models retrain on a continuous or near-continuous schedule (sub-daily ideal, weekly maximum). Monthly retraining or less is not Real AI.
  3. The product would fundamentally break if the ML layer were removed. The ML is doing the actual work, not decorating workflow.
  4. Public technical documentation describes model architecture, training methodology, or both with enough specificity to be falsifiable.

Examples in the current directory: Albert AI, Groas.ai, Persado.

Hybrid — some real ML, some rules

Tools earn Hybrid when:

  • One or more product modules are genuinely ML-driven (audience modeling, copy generation, creative scoring), but
  • The bulk of day-to-day product behavior is rule-based or workflow-based, and
  • Removing the ML modules would degrade the product but not break it.

Examples: Smartly.io, Madgicx, Phrasee, Trapica, Pattern89.

Marketing AI — rule-based product, AI label

The Marketing AI tag is honest naming, not a slur. Tools land here when:

  • The core product is a rule engine, template engine, or a thin wrapper over a foundation model (GPT, Claude), and
  • The “AI” positioning is applied to the whole product when only one feature genuinely qualifies, or
  • The vendor cannot, when asked specifically, describe their model architecture or training approach.

Examples: Anyword, Jasper, Adzooma, WordStream.

The diagnostic questions

When I’m on a vendor sales call (which I’ve done for every tool in this directory), these are the questions I run through. Vendor answers determine the classification more than any marketing copy ever does:

  1. Is the model trained on my account’s data, or on aggregated customer data? Custom-per-account is Real AI. Shared models are Hybrid at best.
  2. How often does the model retrain? Hourly to daily is Real AI. Weekly is Hybrid. Monthly or less is not really machine learning.
  3. What happens to the product if I disable the AI features? If the product still works fully, the AI is decoration.
  4. Can your engineering team describe the model architecture in technical terms? Real AI vendors can answer. Marketing AI vendors deflect to feature lists.
  5. What public documentation describes how the model works? Whitepaper, blog post, technical FAQ. If there’s nothing public, the AI claim is unverifiable.
  6. How is model performance measured against a non-AI baseline? Real AI tools have a benchmark; Marketing AI tools have anecdotes.

Sources I cross-reference

  • Vendor technical documentation — whitepapers, engineering blogs, knowledge-base articles describing model behavior. The presence of detail is informative; the absence is even more so.
  • Sales engineering responses — sales engineers (not account executives) tend to be honest about what their tool actually does, especially when the rubric questions are specific.
  • Support team admissions — opening a support ticket that asks “what model is this feature using and how often is it retrained” is the most consistent way to find out.
  • Public job listings — vendors hiring ML engineers are doing ML; vendors hiring sales engineers and not ML engineers are doing rules.
  • Peer-reviewed research — rare but decisive when present (Persado has it; almost no one else does).

What this classifier won’t do

  • Tell you a tool is bad. A Marketing AI tool can still be useful at the right price. The rating tells you what you’re buying, not whether to buy it.
  • Predict ROI. Real AI doesn’t guarantee outcomes. Plenty of well-engineered Hybrid tools deliver more measurable lift than over-priced Real AI tools.
  • Stay static. Tools move between categories. Acquisitions reduce ML investment; product launches increase it. Quarterly reviews catch the drift.

Reclassifications, dated

  • Pattern89: Real AI → Hybrid (Q4 2025). Reduced documentation specificity post-acquisition; team departures.
  • Madgicx: Marketing AI → Hybrid (Q1 2026). Shipped genuine ML modules in audience and creative.
  • Phrasee: Real AI → Hybrid (Q1 2026). Increased reliance on third-party foundation models.
